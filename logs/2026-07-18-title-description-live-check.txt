lly work</h2><p class="mt-3 text-slate-600">At every well-baby visit, your pediatrician measures three things: weight, 
length and head circumference. Each measurement is plotted on a growth chart — a set of curves built from thousands of 
healthy babies. The curves are labeled with percentiles: the 3rd, 15th, 50th, 85th and 97th are the common reference li
nes. The 50th percentile is the median, the exact middle. If your baby&#x27;s weight lands on the 50th line, half of ba
bies their age weigh more and half weigh less.</p><p class="mt-3 text-slate-600">Here is the single most important idea

 the same way some adults are 5&#x27;2&quot; and others are 6&#x27;2&quot;.      What pediatricians actually watch is t
he <strong>trend</strong>. A baby who has tracked the 25th percentile since birth is the picture of healthy, steady gro
wth. The thing that gets a doctor&#x27;s attention is a baby who jumps or drops across several percentile lines between
 visits, because that change — not the number — can be worth investigating.</p><p class="mt-3 text-slate-600">Measureme

 make decisions. It cannot replace the trained eye and accurate scale at your pediatrician&#x27;s office.</p><p class="
